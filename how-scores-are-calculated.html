Methodology

How scores are calculated

Every score Amplify produces is grounded in established research, published standards, and real conversion data. Here is what we measure, how we measure it, and the sources behind each signal.

How to read your score

Amplify scores are directional indicators grounded in established research and the collective expertise of Automattic. They are not definitive verdicts. A score reflects a point-in-time audit of your homepage and may not account for all site-specific context, audience nuances, or intentional design decisions.

Modifying your website based on Amplify recommendations is done at your own discretion and risk. Amplify does not guarantee specific outcomes as a result of acting on its recommendations. This includes changes to traffic, conversion rates, search rankings, or client enquiries.

Some signals in this tool are generated through AI analysis. These scores carry a higher degree of interpretive variance than programmatic checks and should be treated accordingly.

Trust Signals
18 pts

Does this site give a potential client enough evidence to feel safe handing over a project? All signals are evaluated on the homepage only. A link to a dedicated page counts as sufficient evidence.

72% of consumers find testimonials more credible than brand claims
Testimonials drive a 34% increase in conversions on sales pages
97% of consumers say reviews impact their purchasing decisions
5 or more reviews makes a service 270% more likely to convert
Above-fold award placement outperforms footer placement by 40 to 60% in A/B tests
Signals measured
  • Programmatic
    Testimonials: present on or linked from the homepage. Attributed testimonials with a name, company, and photo score highest; anonymous quotes score lower. Peer experience outweighs brand claims: 72% of consumers say testimonials are more credible than marketing copy, and a real person vouching for you removes doubt that nothing you write about yourself can.
    3 pts
  • Programmatic
    Case studies: link or reference to case study content on the homepage. Quality signal: presence of quantified outcomes. Look for a problem, a solution, and a result with numbers. Specific results are harder to fabricate and far more convincing than claims. An agency that can show they increased a client's revenue by 40% is inherently more trustworthy than one that claims they "deliver results."
    3 pts
  • Programmatic
    Client logos: any client logos present on the homepage. Logos borrow trust from recognized brands. Seeing a client a visitor recognizes immediately elevates the agency's credibility and answers the unspoken question every prospect has: has anyone I have heard of trusted these people?
    2 pts
  • Programmatic
    Results and metrics: any quantified outcomes mentioned on the homepage, such as "increased conversions by 40%." Numbers are harder to fake and easier to believe than adjectives. They give a potential client something concrete to hold onto rather than another generic claim about quality.
    2 pts
  • Programmatic
    Social proof volume: existence of reviews or ratings. Five or more is the threshold where trust begins to compound. A single glowing review is forgettable, but a pattern of positive reviews is persuasive. Research consistently shows that volume of social proof matters as much as the quality of individual reviews.
    2 pts
  • Programmatic
    Awards and recognition: any awards present on the homepage. Third-party validation signals that the agency's quality has been evaluated by someone other than themselves. Placement is also scored: above-fold visibility can outperform footer-only placement by 40 to 60% in A/B tests.
    2 pts
  • Programmatic
    Team or About page: a link or blurb on the homepage is sufficient. Real people with names and faces signal legitimacy and make the agency feel approachable. Faceless agencies are harder to trust. Showing who is behind the work reduces the perceived risk of reaching out.
    2 pts
  • Programmatic
    Years in business or client count: any mention on the homepage counts. Longevity signals stability and reduces the perceived risk of hiring an unproven outfit. Even a modest track record builds confidence when stated directly. "150 clients" or "founded in 2012" gives a visitor something to anchor their trust on.
    2 pts

Contact & Conversion
15 pts

A potential client has made it through the site and is ready to reach out. Does the site make that easy, or does it create friction at the worst possible moment?

Reducing a form from 11 to 4 fields increases conversions by 120%
Action-oriented CTAs convert 121% more than passive ones ("Learn More," "Submit")
First-person CTA phrasing boosts click-through by 90%
Above-fold CTAs outperform below-fold by 304%
Personalized CTAs convert 202% better than generic defaults
Signals measured via DOM analysis
  • Programmatic
    CTA above the fold: at least one clear call-to-action is visible without scrolling. A motivated visitor should not have to scroll to act. Above-fold CTAs outperform below-fold by up to 304%, and every pixel of scroll is a potential exit point for someone who arrived ready to engage.
    3 pts
  • Programmatic Context-dependent
    Form field count: fewer fields reduces friction. The research threshold is 4 or fewer for maximum conversion, but project inquiry forms reasonably require more context. Every additional field is a reason not to complete the form. Reducing from 11 to 4 fields has been shown to increase conversions by 120%.
    3 pts
  • Programmatic
    Contact information present: email address, phone number, or contact form visible on or linked from the homepage. If a potential client is ready to reach out and cannot easily find how, they will not. Removing that friction is the single most direct path to converting a site visit into an enquiry.
    2 pts
  • Programmatic
    CTA repetition: a call-to-action also appears near the bottom of the page. Not every visitor reads linearly. A CTA near the bottom catches the visitor who scrolled through the entire page but did not act at the top, tying back to the Peak-End Rule in Design and Experience.
    2 pts
⚠️ Form field count is context-dependent. Fewer fields is not always better. A client requesting a project estimate expects to provide more detail than a newsletter subscriber. Use this signal directionally.
Signals measured via AI analysis
  • AI
    CTA clarity: is it obvious what happens when you click? Vague CTAs like "Learn more" create decision paralysis, as they could mean anything. Action-oriented, specific copy like "Start your project" or "Get a proposal" sets clear expectations, reduces uncertainty, and motivates action. Action-oriented CTAs convert 121% more than passive ones.
    3 pts
  • AI
    Response time promise: does the site mention how quickly they respond? Any commitment, such as "we will get back to you within 24 hours," reduces the perceived risk of reaching out. Uncertainty about what happens after you submit a form is one of the most underappreciated sources of conversion friction.
    1 pt
  • AI
    Friction language near CTAs: does anything adjacent to the CTA create hesitation? Requiring account creation before contact, the absence of a privacy reassurance near a form, or ambiguous commitment language all reduce completion rates. Even a well-designed CTA can be undermined by a single friction-creating sentence nearby.
    1 pt

SEO
13 pts

Can a potential client find this agency when they search? All SEO signals are sourced directly from Google Search Central documentation.

Signals measured via DOM analysis
  • Programmatic
    H1 and heading hierarchy: one H1 present, with a logical H1 to H2 to H3 structure beneath it. This is a discoverability check. The H1 tells search engines what the page is fundamentally about, and the presence of a heading hierarchy signals that the content is organized rather than a single undifferentiated block. Note: this signal checks that a heading structure exists and that the H1 is clear. Whether that structure is used well for readability is measured in Content Quality. Whether it conforms to WCAG requirements is measured in Accessibility.
    3 pts
  • Programmatic
    Title tag: present, unique, under 60 characters, and keyword-relevant. The title tag is the first thing both search engines and users read in results. It must accurately communicate what the page is about in language a potential client would recognize and click, staying within 60 characters so it is not truncated.
    2 pts
  • Programmatic
    Meta description: present and under 160 characters. While not a direct ranking factor, a well-written meta description significantly influences click-through rate. It is often the only copy a potential client reads before deciding whether to visit. A weak or missing meta description wastes that opportunity entirely.
    2 pts
  • Programmatic
    Image alt text: meaningful images have descriptive alt attributes. Also feeds the Accessibility score. Alt text makes images readable to search crawlers and screen readers alike. Missing alt text means search engines see blank spaces where your best visual evidence, such as case study screenshots, team photos, and client logos, should be.
    2 pts
  • Programmatic
    robots.txt and noindex: the page is not accidentally blocking crawlers. Misconfigured robots.txt or a stray noindex tag is one of the most common and damaging SEO errors. Either can silently remove a page from search results entirely. These are set once and often forgotten, making them a high-value check.
    2 pts
  • Programmatic
    Canonical tag: self-referencing canonical present. Canonicals prevent search engines from splitting ranking signals across duplicate or near-duplicate URLs. Without one, your own site can inadvertently compete against itself. For example, the HTTP and HTTPS versions of a page each accrue partial authority instead of one strong signal.
    1 pt
  • Programmatic
    Structured data: any schema markup present on the homepage. Schema gives crawlers an unambiguous, machine-readable description of the site's content, reducing the chance of misrepresentation in AI-generated summaries and search results. This signal also feeds the AI Mode score.
    1 pt
  • Programmatic
    Intrusive interstitials: pop-ups or overlays that block content on mobile. Google actively penalizes these in mobile rankings. They are also one of the most common reasons visitors bounce immediately, particularly on mobile, where a full-screen overlay covering the content a user just clicked to see is a near-universal abandonment trigger. This signal also feeds the Mobile Experience score.
Signals measured via AI content analysis
  • AI
    Keyword relevance: does the page content reflect what the agency actually does, in language clients would search for? A site full of insider jargon or generic terms fails to appear in the searches that matter. If the words on the page do not match the words a potential client types into Google, no amount of technical SEO will close the gap.
  • AI
    Thin content: is there enough substance for Google to understand what this page is about? Pages with too little text give search engines almost nothing to parse and rank. Thin content also signals low effort. A strong agency homepage should be able to explain its value, its work, and its clients in enough words for a crawler to form a clear picture.

Mobile Experience
13 pts

A first impression increasingly happens on a phone. Does the site hold up when a potential client pulls it up on their device?

Mobile accounts for 62 to 64% of global web traffic as of 2026
Google minimum touch target: 48x48px with 8px spacing
Minimum body font size: 16px. Below this, iOS Safari auto-zooms.
Signals measured via DOM analysis
  • Programmatic
    Touch targets: minimum 48x48px with 8px spacing per Google guidelines. Tappable elements that are too small or too close together cause mis-taps, frustration, and abandonment, particularly on phones used one-handed. This signal also feeds the Design and Experience score via Fitts's Law.
    4 pts
  • Programmatic
    Font size: body text must meet the 16px minimum. Below this threshold, iOS Safari triggers automatic zoom on form fields, disrupting the reading experience mid-interaction. It also strains readability for anyone on a small screen or with slightly reduced vision, two groups that represent a large share of mobile visitors.
    2 pts
  • Programmatic
    Horizontal overflow: any content that bleeds beyond the viewport width and causes horizontal scrolling is flagged. Horizontal scrolling on mobile is a near-universal signal that the layout was never tested on an actual phone. It is disorienting for users and immediately signals that the site is not properly maintained.
    2 pts
  • Programmatic
    Responsive breakpoints: does the CSS include mobile media queries? Their presence is the baseline signal that someone thought about the mobile experience at all. Their absence often correlates with a layout that breaks on smaller screens. Not guaranteed, but strongly predictive.
    1 pt
ℹ️ Viewport meta tag is not scored. The width=device-width, initial-scale=1 tag is present on over 99% of websites as of 2026. It is effectively universal and no longer a differentiating signal. Amplify verifies its presence but does not include it in scoring. A site missing it would already surface failures across the other signals in this criterion.
Signals measured via AI visual analysis
  • AI
    Intrusive interstitials: pop-ups or overlays that cover content on mobile. These are one of the most common reasons users abandon a page immediately on mobile. Google actively penalizes them, and users almost universally react negatively to content being blocked the moment they arrive on a page. This signal also feeds the SEO score.
    2 pts
  • AI
    Navigation usability: does the desktop nav collapse into something usable on mobile, such as a hamburger menu or drawer? Desktop navigation rarely translates directly to a phone. If the nav is unusable on mobile, a visitor cannot find anything, and they will not stay to figure it out.
    1 pt
  • AI
    Content legibility: does the layout hold up at mobile viewport, or do elements overlap and stack awkwardly? A layout that works perfectly on desktop but breaks on mobile signals incomplete testing. Overlapping text, cropped images, and misaligned sections make the page feel broken, and broken pages do not convert.
    1 pt

Content Quality
12 pts

Is the writing compelling, clear, and professional? Errors and poor readability erode trust before a client has read a single sentence.

97.2% of people say grammar influences their perception of a company
Pages with spelling or grammar errors have 85% higher bounce rates
59% of consumers are less likely to trust a brand with copy errors
Ideal Flesch Reading Ease score for web content: 60 to 70 (8th to 9th grade level)
Signals measured
  • Programmatic
    Flesch Reading Ease score calculated from extracted page text. A score of 60 to 70 targets 8th to 9th grade reading level, widely accessible without being patronizing. Below 50 means dense, tiring prose that potential clients will skim over without absorbing. Readable writing is not dumbing down; it is respecting the reader's time.
    3 pts
  • Programmatic
    Sentence and paragraph length: a proxy for scannability. Long, unbroken walls of text discourage reading before a single word is processed. Short sentences and short paragraphs signal a site that respects how people actually read on screens. Rarely word by word.
    2 pts
  • AI
    Grammar and spelling: AI reads the page copy and flags visible errors. Errors in copy undermine everything else on the page. 59% of consumers say they are less likely to trust a brand with copy errors. A single noticeable typo or grammar mistake can undo dozens of other trust signals in a visitor's mind.
    2 pts
  • Programmatic
    Content volume: is there enough copy for a reader to understand what this agency does, or is the page mostly images? A page that is predominantly visual gives search crawlers and AI tools very little to parse. Sufficient text is the minimum required for the site to be understood, indexed, and cited accurately.
    1 pt
  • Programmatic
    Heading and subheading usage: are H2s and H3s actually used to break the page into readable sections, or is the body copy one uninterrupted block? This is a readability check, not a structure check. A page can have a technically valid heading hierarchy and still fail here if all the content sits under a single H2 with no further subdivision. Visitors scan before they read, and headings are the primary tool they use to decide what is worth reading.
    1 pt
  • AI
    Passive voice density: excessive passive voice weakens authority and clarity. "Results were achieved" is less compelling than "We achieved." Active voice signals confidence and ownership, making the agency the actor rather than a bystander in their own story. High passive voice density correlates with copy that feels evasive.
    1 pt
  • AI
    Buzzword and jargon density: overlaps with Audience Resonance but scores how the copy reads, not who it speaks to. Terms like "synergy," "disruptive," and "results-driven" are so overused they carry no information. They signal padding rather than substance and reduce the overall credibility of the copy around them.
    1 pt
  • AI
    Clarity per section: does each section say something meaningful, or is it filler? Every section should earn its place by communicating something specific. Filler content, sections that look substantive but say nothing new, dilutes the signal-to-noise ratio and makes the overall site feel less considered and less trustworthy.
    1 pt

  • Flesch Kincaid Grade Level (Readable)

Design & Experience
11 pts

Does the site look credible and feel effortless to use? All signals in this criterion are grounded in the Laws of UX, a framework of established psychological and behavioral principles applied to interface design.

Signals measured via DOM analysis
  • Programmatic
    Hick's Law: decision time increases with the number of choices. Nav item count, visible CTAs, and form fields are checked, and menus exceeding seven items are flagged. Too many choices slow decisions and cause visitors to leave without acting. A cleaner, more focused set of options gives visitors a clearer path to conversion.
    1 pt
  • Programmatic
    Fitts's Law: CTA button dimensions, touch target sizes, and spacing between clickable elements. Small or crowded targets require more precision to click or tap, and mis-taps on mobile are frustrating. Button size directly affects conversion rate, particularly on mobile where fingers replace pointers. This signal also feeds the Mobile Experience score.
    1 pt
  • Programmatic Context-dependent
    Jakob's Law: users expect sites to work like other sites. Standard pattern conformance is checked, including logo placement, nav position, primary CTA, and footer links. Visitors spend most of their time on other sites, so they bring those expectations to yours. Deviating from familiar patterns creates unnecessary cognitive friction that costs conversions.
    1 pt
  • Programmatic
    Serial Position Effect: the first and last items in a series are best remembered. Key content placement in nav, hero, and page structure is evaluated. What you put first and last gets remembered. Key messages and CTAs placed at the start and end of sections have higher recall than those buried in the middle.
    1 pt
  • Programmatic
    Miller's Law: working memory holds approximately seven items. Elements competing for attention in key zones (nav, hero, footer) are counted and flagged when excessive. When too many elements vie for attention simultaneously, none of them register. This is a context signal for Hick's Law, not a standalone scoring criterion.
⚠️ Jakob's Law is context-dependent. Agencies with intentionally non-standard designs should not be penalized the same as those with oversight-driven deviations. Use this signal as a directional indicator, not a verdict.
Signals measured via AI visual analysis
  • AI
    Aesthetic-Usability Effect: beautiful design is perceived as more usable. Overall visual polish scored from a screenshot. Visually polished design builds immediate trust and is perceived as more capable, even before a word is read. Low visual quality is often the unconscious reason a visitor bounces within the first five seconds.
    2 pts
  • AI
    Von Restorff Effect: the element that stands out gets remembered. Does the primary CTA visually differentiate from everything around it? If the CTA does not stand out from the surrounding content, visitors cannot act on it even when they want to. Differentiation drives attention, and attention drives clicks.
    2 pts
  • AI Context-dependent
    Peak-End Rule: people judge an experience by its most intense moment and its ending. The hero (the peak) and the final CTA or footer (the end) are evaluated. A weak hero or a poorly considered footer anchor can drag down the overall impression of a visit, even if the middle sections are strong.
    2 pts
  • AI
    Gestalt Laws (Proximity, Common Region, Similarity, Uniform Connectedness, Praegnanz): are related elements grouped? Is the layout clean or cluttered? Scored as one composite signal. Cluttered or ungrouped layouts make pages harder to process and feel less credible. Clear visual grouping helps visitors understand page structure instantly, without having to think about it.
    1 pt
⚠️ Peak-End Rule is context-dependent. This signal assumes the hero is the site's strongest moment, which is not always the case. Use the score directionally and consider what your actual peak moment is.
Sources
  • Laws of UX (lawsofux.com)

Accessibility
10 pts

Does the site work for everyone, regardless of how they experience the web? Amplify measures against the WCAG AA conformance level, the widely accepted standard referenced by courts and regulators globally.

Signals measured via DOM analysis
  • Programmatic
    Image alt text WCAG 1.1.1: all meaningful images have descriptive alt attributes. Screen readers read alt text aloud to visually impaired users. Without it, meaningful images are completely invisible to them. Alt text also gives crawlers richer context about the page's content. Also feeds SEO.
    2 pts
  • Programmatic
    Color contrast ratio WCAG 1.4.3: minimum 4.5:1 for normal text, 3:1 for large text. Low contrast makes text hard to read for anyone with reduced vision, in bright sunlight, or on a lower-quality screen. The WCAG AA threshold is a legal standard in many jurisdictions, not just a best practice.
    2 pts
  • Programmatic
    Form labels WCAG 1.3.1: all input fields have associated labels. Unlabeled form fields break the experience for screen reader users entirely. Without a label, they cannot tell what a field is asking. Labels also reduce ambiguity for all users, which reduces form abandonment.
    1 pt
  • Programmatic
    Language attribute WCAG 3.1.1: <html lang=""> present so screen readers identify the language. Without it, a French-language site might be read aloud with English pronunciation, making it incomprehensible to its intended audience. A one-attribute fix that affects every screen reader user on the page.
    1 pt
  • Programmatic
    Skip navigation link WCAG 2.4.1: allows keyboard users to skip past repeated nav. Without a skip link, keyboard users must tab through every navigation item on every page load, a slow, frustrating barrier that affects all keyboard-only users, not just those using screen readers.
    1 pt
  • Programmatic
    No auto-playing media WCAG 1.4.2: audio or video that plays without user action. Unexpected audio startles users, is disruptive in quiet environments, and is particularly disorienting for screen reader users who suddenly cannot hear their own tool. Auto-playing video also drains mobile data without consent.
    1 pt
  • Programmatic
    Heading structure WCAG 1.3.1: heading levels must not skip. Jumping from H1 directly to H3, or using multiple H1s, is a WCAG violation. This is a conformance check, not a readability check. Screen reader users navigate a page by jumping between headings, and skipped levels break that navigation model entirely. A page can use headings generously for scannability (Content Quality) and still fail here if the levels are out of sequence.
    1 pt
Signals measured via AI visual analysis
  • AI
    Focus indicators WCAG 2.4.7: are interactive elements visually distinguishable when focused? Users who navigate by keyboard need to see which element is currently focused. Hidden focus rings, often removed by CSS resets, make a page effectively unusable for keyboard-only navigation.
    1 pt
  • Phase 2
    Keyboard navigability: full keyboard-only navigation testing requires interaction and will be added in a future release.
Sources
  • WCAG, Web Content Accessibility Guidelines (W3C)

Audience Resonance

Does this site feel made for the right client? Within seconds of landing, a potential client should feel like the site is speaking directly to them.

First impressions form in as little as 50 milliseconds
Users give a homepage 3 to 5 seconds to earn trust
75% of consumers judge credibility based on website design alone
Specific, targeted messaging converts 202% better than generic copy

Signals measured
  • AI
    5-second clarity test: can an AI reading the homepage answer what this agency does, who they serve, and why a client should care? Visitors decide whether to stay within 3 to 5 seconds. If the page cannot answer those three questions in that window, the opportunity is lost, regardless of how strong the rest of the site is.
    2 pts
  • AI
    Audience specificity: does the copy call out a specific type of client, industry, or pain point rather than speaking to everyone? Specific messaging converts 202% better than generic. "We build WooCommerce stores for DTC brands" creates immediate recognition in the right visitor. That "that's me" feeling is something no generic copy can replicate.
    2 pts
  • AI
    Pain point language: does the homepage reflect the actual problems potential clients have, in the words they would use? When a visitor reads copy that names their exact problem in their own language, they feel understood. That moment of recognition is what turns a casual browse into a genuine enquiry.
    1 pt
  • AI
    Generic claim detection: hollow buzzwords like "results-driven," "passionate," and "full-service," with no supporting specifics, lower the score. These phrases describe every agency equally, which means they differentiate none of them. Generic claims are the single most common reason an agency homepage fails to convert despite looking professional.
    1 pt
  • AI
    Hero headline clarity: does the H1 communicate the value proposition immediately, or is it vague? The H1 is the single highest-attention element on the page. A vague H1 like "Welcome to our agency" wastes that attention entirely. A specific one like "We build WooCommerce stores for independent brands" earns the next scroll.
    1 pt
  • Programmatic
    Above-fold content presence: is there meaningful text above the fold, or only a full-screen image or video with no copy? A page that is all visual and no text gives visitors nothing to evaluate in the critical first seconds, and gives search engines and AI tools nothing to index. Meaningful above-fold copy is the minimum signal that this page exists to communicate something.
    1 pt

Technical Health
20 pts

Can AI tools access, crawl, and render your site? If a crawler cannot reach your content, nothing else matters. This criterion covers both accessibility and raw performance.

HTTPS has been a confirmed Google ranking signal since 2014
A misconfigured robots.txt can silently block all AI crawlers from ever reaching your content
Sites that block GPTBot, ClaudeBot, or anthropic-ai are invisible to AI-generated answers
Pages rendered entirely in JavaScript are not reliably indexed by most AI crawlers
Signals measured
  • Programmatic
    robots.txt: present and not blocking AI crawlers (GPTBot, ClaudeBot, anthropic-ai) alongside standard Googlebot. Many sites use blanket Disallow rules that were never intended to block AI crawlers but do. If crawlers cannot reach the content, no other optimization matters. This is the most fundamental gate in AI Mode.
    7 pts
  • Programmatic
    noindex: the homepage and key content pages are not flagged to prevent indexing. A noindex on the homepage is almost always a development-environment mistake that made it to production. The result is immediate and severe: the page is completely absent from search and AI crawl queues.
    4 pts
  • Programmatic
    HTTPS: SSL active with no mixed content warnings. SSL is not just a trust signal for visitors. It is a prerequisite for Google ranking. Mixed content warnings mean parts of the page are served over HTTP even on an HTTPS site, triggering browser security warnings that erode trust immediately.
    4 pts
  • Programmatic
    Sitemap: present, linked in robots.txt, with valid lastmod dates so crawlers know what has been updated. Without accurate lastmod dates, crawlers guess which content to re-index and may skip recently updated pages entirely. A sitemap is how you tell crawlers what matters and when it changed.
    3 pts
  • Programmatic
    JavaScript dependency: critical content readable in raw HTML, not locked behind client-side rendering that crawlers cannot execute. Many AI crawlers and some search bots do not execute JavaScript. If your core content only renders after JS runs, crawlers see a blank page. Static-first content is the safe baseline for discoverability.
    2 pts

Structured Data
18 pts

Schema markup tells AI tools exactly what your site is about rather than making them infer it. It is the difference between an AI accurately describing your agency and producing a generic summary.

Sites with structured data get an average 20 to 30% more clicks in search results
FAQ schema can take up to 50% more space in search results, increasing visibility
JSON-LD is Google's preferred structured data format
Signals measured
  • Programmatic
    Organization schema: name, URL, logo, description, and sameAs social profiles marked up in JSON-LD. Without it, AI tools have to infer who you are from raw HTML. With it, they get an unambiguous, machine-readable record of your business name, logo, location, and where else you exist on the web.

llms.txt
3 pts

A plain-text file at your site root that gives AI tools a curated, concise summary of what your site contains, written specifically for LLMs rather than humans. Think of it as a robots.txt for the AI era.

🌱 Emerging standard. llms.txt was proposed in September 2024 and adoption is still minimal. Absence will not significantly penalize your score, but presence is a meaningful differentiator. We will flag the opportunity with implementation guidance in your report.
Signals measured
  • Programmatic
    llms.txt present: file exists at yourdomain.com/llms.txt with a concise, accurate description of the site. An llms.txt file gives AI tools a curated briefing on what the site contains, written for machines, not humans. It is the fastest way to ensure AI tools understand the agency accurately without having to infer it from raw HTML.
    2 pts
  • Programmatic
    llms-full.txt present: extended version with deeper context about services, team, and expertise. The full version provides richer context for AI tools that do deeper research before generating an answer or recommendation. Its presence signals a higher level of intentional AI-readiness.
    ½ pt
  • AI
    Content quality: if present, does the file accurately and specifically describe the agency, or is it vague boilerplate that adds no signal value? A generic llms.txt is nearly as useless as none at all. The benefit comes entirely from specificity: named services, named team members, named client types, and honest scope of work.
    ½ pt
